## The Pause Continues

The Federal Open Market Committee voted unanimously on February 18, 2026, to maintain the federal funds rate at 4.75-5.00%, extending the policy pause that began in December 2025. The decision, widely anticipated by markets, reflected the Committee's cautious assessment of inflation progress and economic resilience.

Chair Jerome Powell emphasized during the post-meeting press conference that the Fed requires "greater confidence" that inflation is sustainably returning to the 2% target before considering rate reductions. "We've made substantial progress, but the job isn't finished," Powell stated. "Premature easing risks reigniting inflationary pressures."

## Inflation Data Mixed

The Fed's decision followed the release of January's Consumer Price Index, which showed headline inflation at 3.2% year-over-year—unchanged from December but above the 3.0% consensus forecast. Core inflation, excluding food and energy, remained sticky at 3.5%, with services price pressures proving particularly persistent.

Housing costs continue driving inflation, with the shelter index rising 5.8% annually despite moderation in rent growth for new leases. The lag between market rent declines and their reflection in official inflation metrics has complicated the Fed's assessment of underlying price trends.

## Labor Market Shows Resilience

Employment data has reinforced the case for policy patience. Nonfarm payrolls increased by 287,000 in January, substantially exceeding expectations, while the unemployment rate ticked down to 4.0%. Wage growth, while moderating from 2024 peaks, remains above levels consistent with 2% inflation.

The Conference Board's Employment Trends Index suggests continued labor market strength through the first quarter. "Businesses are still hiring despite higher interest rates," observed Conference Board economist Selcuk Eren. "This resilience gives the Fed breathing room to keep policy restrictive."

## Market Reactions

Treasury markets initially sold off following Powell's comments, with 2-year yields rising 12 basis points to 4.45%. However, equity markets recovered from early losses as investors digested the prospect of sustained economic growth accompanying the higher-for-longer rate environment.

The dollar index strengthened 0.8% against major currencies, reflecting both the Fed's relatively hawkish stance compared to other central banks and safe-haven flows amid geopolitical tensions. The euro declined to $1.08, its lowest level since November 2024.

## Global Policy Divergence

The Federal Reserve's cautious approach contrasts with more accommodative stances elsewhere. The European Central Bank cut its deposit rate by 25 basis points in February, citing weaker euro area growth prospects. The Bank of England is widely expected to follow suit at its March meeting.

This divergence has widened interest rate differentials, supporting dollar strength but creating challenges for emerging market economies with dollar-denominated debt. Several Latin American central banks have delayed planned rate cuts to maintain competitiveness against U.S. yields.

## Forward Guidance

The FOMC's statement removed previous language suggesting willingness to tolerate temporary inflation overshoots, signaling increased commitment to price stability. The Committee's dot plot projections indicated a median expectation of 75 basis points of easing during 2026, down from 100 basis points projected in December.

As markets adjust to the prospect of extended restrictive policy, the Fed's challenge remains navigating the narrow path between controlling inflation and avoiding unnecessary economic damage. Powell's emphasis on data-dependency suggests policy decisions will remain highly reactive to incoming economic indicators through the remainder of 2026.

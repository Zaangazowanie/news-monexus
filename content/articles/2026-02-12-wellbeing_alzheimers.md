## Historic Approval

The U.S. Food and Drug Administration granted full approval to Eli Lilly's donanemab on February 10, 2026, marking a significant milestone in Alzheimer's disease treatment. The drug, marketed as Kisunla, becomes the second anti-amyloid antibody approved for early-stage Alzheimer's following Biogen and Eisai's lecanemab approval in 2023.

Donanemab demonstrated a 35% slowing of cognitive decline in clinical trials involving patients with early symptomatic Alzheimer's disease. The treatment works by binding to amyloid plaques in the brain and facilitating their removal, potentially halting disease progression when administered before significant neuronal damage occurs.

## Expanded Access

Full FDA approval removes previous restrictions that limited donanemab to patients enrolled in clinical trials or special access programs. An estimated 6.7 million Americans currently live with Alzheimer's, though only approximately 15-20% are diagnosed at early stages when anti-amyloid therapies prove most effective.

Medicare confirmed it will cover donanemab under Part B, subject to registry requirements tracking real-world effectiveness and side effects. The decision ensures that patients over 65—the population most affected by Alzheimer's—will have access regardless of income.

## Safety Considerations

The approval includes a boxed warning about amyloid-related imaging abnormalities (ARIA), which can cause brain swelling or bleeding. Approximately 24% of trial participants experienced ARIA, though most cases were asymptomatic and resolved without intervention.

FDA reviewers emphasized the importance of regular MRI monitoring during treatment, particularly for patients carrying the APOE4 gene variant, which increases both Alzheimer's risk and ARIA susceptibility. Treatment centers must demonstrate capability to manage these complications before prescribing donanemab.

## Healthcare System Impact

The approval presents significant implementation challenges for healthcare systems. Donanemab requires monthly intravenous infusions over 18 months, followed by periodic monitoring. Each infusion session lasts approximately 30 minutes, creating capacity constraints for infusion centers already managing oncology and immunology patients.

The Alzheimer's Association has called for federal funding to expand specialized treatment centers in underserved regions. Currently, 43 U.S. counties lack neurologists, forcing patients to travel hundreds of miles for diagnosis and treatment.

## Cost and Access Debates

Eli Lilly has priced donanemab at $32,000 annually—substantially below the $26,500 price of competitor lecanemab when adjusted for treatment duration. However, total costs including infusion administration, monitoring, and management of side effects may reach $90,000 per patient annually.

Patient advocacy groups have praised the pricing relative to clinical benefit but emphasized that cost-sharing requirements could still exclude uninsured or underinsured patients. The Institute for Clinical and Economic Review estimates that a fair price would be approximately $28,000 annually based on quality-adjusted life years gained.

## Research Implications

Donanemab's approval reinforces the amyloid hypothesis—the theory that amyloid plaque accumulation drives Alzheimer's pathology—that had been questioned following numerous failed drug trials. However, researchers caution that amyloid removal alone may be insufficient for patients with advanced disease.

Combination therapies targeting multiple disease mechanisms are now entering clinical trials. Researchers are particularly interested in pairing anti-amyloid drugs with anti-tau agents, potentially addressing both protein abnormalities characteristic of Alzheimer's pathology.

## Patient and Caregiver Hope

For Alzheimer's patients and their families, donanemab's approval offers tangible hope after decades of failed drug development. While not a cure, the therapy represents the first class of treatments that modify disease progression rather than merely addressing symptoms.

Early diagnosis will be critical to realizing donanemab's potential benefits. Healthcare providers are expected to increase screening for mild cognitive impairment, potentially identifying patients who might benefit from intervention before irreversible brain damage occurs.

# Lead

"When I enter a museum, I'm not looking for what's on the walls, I'm looking at what's behind them," says a Native American woman in the Sundance documentary *Aanikoobijigan*. "I wonder if people know that they've paid $20 to enter a museum that's holding our ancestors hostage."

# The Film

- Premiered at Sundance 2026
- Documents Michigan State University's dispute with Michigan Anishinaabek Cultural Preservation and Repatriation Alliance (MACPRA)
- Exposes scale of Indigenous remains still held in US institutions
- Directors: Adam Khalil & Zach Khalil

# The Story Arc

- **Origin:** Jefferson's 1784 mound excavation — "first American archaeologist" = first grave robber
- **Legal:** NAGPRA 1990 promised repatriation. 35 years later, thousands remain in storage
- **Resolution:** Film shows re-burial ritual — what justice looks like

# What's Being Hidden

- How many ancestors are still in storage? (Thousands across hundreds of institutions)
- Why does repatriation take decades? (Bureaucracy, institutional resistance)
- What's the legal framework? (NAGPRA exists but enforcement is weak)

# Incentive Mapping

- **Museums:** Research collections, prestige, liability avoidance
- **Universities:** Academic freedom arguments, bureaucratic inertia
- **Indigenous communities:** Want their ancestors back — period

# Critical Questions

1. Why are institutions still holding remains 30+ years after NAGPRA?
2. What happens to institutions that refuse to comply?
3. How many "research specimens" are actually stolen ancestors?

# Kicker

The documentary isn't about art repatriation. It's about human remains — and the institutions that still treat Indigenous bodies as property.

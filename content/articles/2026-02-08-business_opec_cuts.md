## Supply Restrictions Continue

The OPEC+ alliance announced on February 5, 2026, that it would extend crude oil production cuts totaling 2.2 million barrels per day through the second quarter of 2026. The decision, reached at a virtual ministerial meeting, sent Brent crude prices above $85 per barrel for the first time since October 2024, with West Texas Intermediate approaching $82.

Saudi Energy Minister Prince Abdulaziz bin Salman emphasized the group's commitment to market stability, stating that OPEC+ remained "proactive and preemptive" in managing supply. The kingdom will continue shouldering the largest burden of cuts, with output limited to 9 million barrels daily—approximately 1.3 million below its theoretical production capacity.

## Market Tightens

The extension of cuts comes as global oil inventories have declined to five-year lows. The International Energy Agency estimates that the market deficit averaged 1.8 million barrels per day during the fourth quarter of 2025, with stock draws accelerating in January as winter heating demand peaked in the Northern Hemisphere.

U.S. shale producers have failed to respond to higher prices with significant production increases, constrained by investor demands for capital discipline and operational challenges in the Permian Basin's most productive zones. American output has plateaued near 13.2 million barrels per day despite WTI prices near $80.

## Consumer Impact

Rising crude prices have translated to higher fuel costs for consumers. The American Automobile Association reports that average U.S. gasoline prices have increased to $3.45 per gallon, up from $3.12 in November 2025. Diesel prices have climbed more sharply, reaching $4.20 per gallon and increasing transportation costs across the economy.

Airlines have begun implementing fuel surcharges on international routes, with United Airlines announcing a $25 increase on transatlantic fares. FedEx and UPS have raised ground shipping rates, citing diesel costs as a significant factor.

## Geopolitical Premium

Oil prices include a substantial geopolitical risk premium reflecting tensions in the Middle East and the ongoing crisis in the Red Sea. While Houthi attacks on shipping have disrupted some cargo movements, most oil traffic has rerouted around the Cape of Good Hope, adding 10-14 days to delivery times but avoiding the Suez Canal.

Analysts estimate that $8-12 of current prices reflects supply security concerns rather than fundamental supply-demand dynamics. Any escalation in the Israel-Hamas conflict or broader regional tensions could push prices substantially higher.

## Demand Outlook

Despite price increases, global oil demand continues growing, driven by emerging market consumption. The IEA projects 2026 demand will average 104.2 million barrels per day, up 1.4 million from 2025. Chinese consumption has rebounded following the country's 2025 economic stimulus measures, with imports averaging 11.3 million barrels daily in January.

Electric vehicle adoption, while accelerating, has not yet meaningfully displaced oil demand. Global EV sales reached 22% of new vehicle purchases in 2025, but the existing fleet of internal combustion vehicles ensures continued petroleum dependence for years to come.

## Investment Implications

Energy sector equities have outperformed broader markets in 2026, with the S&P 500 Energy index gaining 18% year-to-date versus 3% for the overall index. ExxonMobil and Chevron have announced increased share buyback programs, redirecting windfall cash flows to shareholders rather than aggressive production expansion.

As OPEC+ maintains supply discipline and demand continues growing, oil markets appear poised for sustained tightness. Consumers and energy-intensive industries should prepare for elevated prices through at least mid-2026.

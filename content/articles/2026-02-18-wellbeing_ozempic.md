## Landmark Trial Results

Novo Nordisk announced results from the SELECT cardiovascular outcomes trial on February 15, 2026, demonstrating that semaglutide (Ozempic) reduced major adverse cardiovascular events by 20% in adults with overweight or obesity and established heart disease. The findings, published simultaneously in the New England Journal of Medicine, establish GLP-1 receptor agonists as cardiometabolic drugs with benefits extending far beyond weight management.

The five-year study enrolled 17,604 participants across 41 countries, making it one of the largest cardiovascular trials conducted. Participants receiving semaglutide experienced significant reductions in heart attacks, strokes, and cardiovascular deaths compared to those receiving placebo, regardless of baseline weight or diabetes status.

## Beyond Weight Loss

While semaglutide's weight loss effects are well-documented, cardiovascular benefits exceeded what researchers expected based on weight reduction alone. Participants lost an average of 15% of body weight, but cardiovascular risk reduction was disproportionately large relative to other weight-loss interventions.

"This suggests GLP-1 drugs have direct cardioprotective effects independent of weight loss," explained Dr. Michael Lincoff, the trial's lead investigator. "We're seeing improvements in endothelial function, reductions in inflammatory markers, and beneficial changes in lipid profiles that contribute to cardiovascular protection."

## Implications for Prescribing

The results are expected to dramatically expand GLP-1 drug prescribing. Current guidelines recommend these medications primarily for diabetes management and weight loss in patients with BMI above 30. The SELECT data support use in patients with cardiovascular disease regardless of weight, potentially qualifying millions of additional patients.

Insurance coverage decisions will be critical to access. Medicare currently covers GLP-1 drugs only for diabetes treatment, requiring beneficiaries to pay out-of-pocket for weight-loss indications—costs exceeding $1,000 monthly. Cardiovascular indication may prompt reconsideration of coverage policies.

## Safety Profile Confirmed

The trial confirmed previously known side effects including nausea, diarrhea, and gallbladder-related events. Gastrointestinal symptoms led to discontinuation in 6.5% of semaglutide recipients versus 1.5% in the placebo group.

Notably, the study found no increased risk of pancreatitis or thyroid cancer, concerns that had emerged from animal studies and post-marketing surveillance. Rates of these events were similar between treatment and placebo groups across the large patient population.

## Healthcare Economics

Cardiovascular disease costs the United States approximately $400 billion annually in direct medical expenses and lost productivity. A 20% reduction in major events among eligible patients could generate savings exceeding $50 billion annually, according to analysis by the American Heart Association.

However, drug costs present a significant barrier. Semaglutide's list price of $935 monthly translates to over $56,000 for five years of treatment—far exceeding potential savings from prevented cardiovascular events for individual patients. Population-level health economic models suggest cost-effectiveness thresholds would require 40-50% price reductions.

## Competitor Response

Eli Lilly's tirzepatide (Mounjaro/Zepbound), which targets both GLP-1 and GIP receptors, is expected to demonstrate similar cardiovascular benefits when SURPASS-CVOT trial results are released later this year. Early data suggest potentially greater weight loss and glycemic control, which might translate to enhanced cardioprotection.

Pfizer and AstraZeneca are developing oral GLP-1 medications that could improve accessibility and reduce costs compared to injectable formulations. These products, if approved, might address the manufacturing constraints that have limited supply of existing drugs.

## Patient Access Challenges

Despite compelling data, patient access to GLP-1 drugs remains severely constrained by supply shortages. Novo Nordisk has stated that manufacturing capacity will not meet demand until late 2026 at earliest, forcing physicians to prioritize patients with highest cardiovascular risk.

The SELECT trial results represent a paradigm shift in cardiovascular prevention, establishing obesity pharmacotherapy as a cornerstone of heart disease management. Realizing these benefits at population scale will require addressing manufacturing, pricing, and distribution challenges that currently limit access.

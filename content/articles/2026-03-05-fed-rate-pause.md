# Lead

Cleveland Fed President Beth Hammack says the Fed should hold interest rates steady for "quite some time," citing persistent inflation and the Iran war as new inflationary risks. But the pause isn't caution — it's what happens when the Fed doesn't know what to do.

# What Hammack Said

- Supports holding rates steady for "quite some time"
- Concerned about persistent inflation
- Wants clear evidence inflation is retreating before cuts
- Iran war represents new inflationary risk
- Voted AGAINST December 2024 rate cut (lone dissenter)

# What's Actually Happening

- Fed already cut rates 175 bps over 18 months (as of Feb 2026)
- Unemployment projected to hit 4.5% in 2026 (Deloitte)
- Consumer spending slowing to 1.6% from 2.6% (Deloitte)
- Inflation still above 2% target

# The Pattern

- Fed cuts rates when inflation falls
- Pauses when uncertainty rises
- But never acts *before* damage shows up in data
- Always reactive, never proactive

# Incentive Mapping

- **Fed:** Needs to appear data-driven (hence "wait and see")
- **Markets:** Want certainty (getting mixed signals)
- **Workers:** Facing 4.5% unemployment regardless of Fed's pace
- **Economy:** Slowing regardless of rate policy

# Critical Questions

1. If the Fed is "data-driven," why does it always lag the data?
2. Does "pausing" during uncertainty help, or just delay the inevitable?
3. Who benefits from the Fed's reactive stance vs who gets left behind?

# Kicker

The Fed doesn't lead the economy — it follows damage reports. "Data-driven" = "we wait for things to break, then we respond."
